# slide_03_customer_profile

[Transition] To design an effective programme, we need to understand who our buyer is.

The picture is remarkably clear. [Pause]

[Data] 48% of Kimsoong buyers are under 30 years old. 82% are middle income. 75% are in employment. This is a mainstream, digitally-active segment — exactly the audience most responsive to loyalty incentives, digital engagement, and value-driven rewards.

[Benchmark] Compared to the difficulty of reaching premium or older demographics, this segment is low-cost to retain: they respond to clear financial value, convenience, and brand trust.

The implication for programme design: keep it simple, keep it financial, make it easy to claim. Economy and price are their top two priorities — our loyalty initiatives must speak directly to that.

Key points: ① Core buyer = young, middle-income, employed ② Highly responsive audience for cost-effective loyalty incentives ③ Programme design should emphasise economy and financial value

# slide_05_root_cause

[Transition] The matrix showed us the gap. This slide quantifies the root causes of actual defection.

The Pareto principle is at work here. [Pause]

[Data] Two causes — bought a competitor's model at 52%, and disappointment with service at 26% — account for 78% of all customer loss. Add in the 9% "unknown" that we can partially recover through better survey data, and the controllable portion rises further.

[Benchmark] The remaining 22% — relocation and stopping driving — are genuinely uncontrollable. But 78%? That is a solvable problem, not an unavoidable one.

The critical insight for management is this: we do not need to solve everything. We need to solve two things exceptionally well — price competitiveness at repurchase, and service quality. [Pause]

Key points: ① 52% + 26% = 78% controllable loss ② Only two levers needed: price and service ③ 9% unknown is reduceable with better survey data
Duration: 2 minutes

# slide_06_strategic_pillars

[Transition] With the root causes established, we can build the strategic framework. Four pillars. Four measurable outcomes.

Each pillar translates a behavioural objective into a business metric. [Pause]

Pillar one: build long-term relationships. Outcome: customers who buy three to four vehicles over ten years, not just one.

Pillar two: drive loyalty directly. Outcome: repeat rate from 15% to 30% within 12 months.

Pillar three: improve data quality. [Data] We currently have only 40% survey return rate. The target is 70% — enabling better buyer profiling and smarter product decisions.

Pillar four: staff engagement. Outcome: service rating from 33% "good or above" to 60%. [Benchmark] This is nearly double the current satisfaction level.

[Scan Room] Each pillar has a clear owner and a clear KPI. This is an accountable framework — not aspirational language.

Key points: ① Four pillars, four measurable KPIs ② 15% → 30% repeat rate is the primary commercial target ③ Staff engagement and data quality are the enablers

# slide_07_initiatives

[Transition] Four pillars. Five initiatives to deliver them. Prioritised by impact-to-cost ratio.

Initiatives one and two are the must-win battles. [Pause]

[Data] Initiative one: three years free after-sales service after every purchase. This directly targets the 26% of defectors who cited service disappointment. It also signals a fundamental shift in Kimsoong's customer commitment.

Initiative two: 20% loyalty discount for returning customers. This addresses the competitive switching problem — the 52% who bought a competitor's model. At a 20% price advantage, the calculus changes.

[Benchmark] Together, these two initiatives address 78% of the controllable defection. The remaining three — trade-in programme, customer magazine, and survey incentive — are low-cost complements at high ROI.

The resource allocation message is simple: concentrate budget on initiatives one and two. Don't dilute the programme across all five equally.

Key points: ① Initiatives 01 and 02 are the core — together they address 78% of losses ② Initiatives 03–05 are high-ROI, low-cost complements ③ Cost shared 50/50 across HQ and franchises

# slide_08_roadmap

[Transition] Finally — how do we deliver this in 12 months?

The roadmap follows a phased logic: prove it works, then scale it. [Pause]

Q1 is about quick wins — magazine and the survey incentive. Low cost, fast to launch, builds brand presence and starts improving our data quality.

Q2 is the core structural reform: the free after-sales service programme. This is the hardest to deliver but the highest impact. [Data] Getting this right is what moves service satisfaction from 33% to 60%.

Q3 activates the financial incentives — loyalty discount and trade-in. This is when we start converting returning customer intent into actual sales.

Q4 measures everything and plans year two.

[Data] The headline targets: repeat purchase rate doubles from 15% to 30%. Service satisfaction rises from 33% to 60%. Survey return rate rises from 40% to 70%. [Scan Room] All three are achievable within 12 months.

Cost structure: 50/50 split between head office and franchises — keeping this affordable at both levels.

Key points: ① Four-phase delivery paced for momentum and manageability ② Q2 delivery of free after-sales is the critical path item ③ Three KPI targets achievable within 12 months at low incremental cost
